
Layer 1: Document identity & structure — the backbone that every other node hangs off.



The document spine (Layer 1)
Think of Paper as the root of everything. It's not just metadata — it's the identity anchor. Every single node in your graph traces back to a Paper. The doi is the globally unique ID (like a social security number for academic papers). abstract_embedding is a vector that lets you find semantically similar papers without reading them. field_of_study is critical for STEM because it scopes retrieval — a query about "neural networks" should not surface papers from civil engineering.
Author hangs off Paper via AUTHORED_BY. The important thing here is orcid — it's a persistent researcher ID that survives name changes, institutional moves, and transliteration differences. Without it, "J. Zhang" from MIT and "J. Zhang" from Peking University are the same node in your graph, which is wrong. h_index lets you weight retrieval by author credibility, which matters in STEM where domain authority is a real signal.
Journal connects via PUBLISHED_IN. impact_factor and open_access are retrieval filters, not decorations. A user asking "what does the latest high-impact research say about X" is implicitly asking you to filter by journal tier. issn is the stable identifier, same reason as orcid — journal names change, ISSNs don't.
Section is where the document becomes navigable. The single most important field is section_type. In a STEM paper, these are almost always: abstract, introduction, related work, methods, experiments, results, discussion, conclusion, supplementary. If you don't tag section type, your graph cannot distinguish "the authors claim their method works" (introduction) from "the authors measured that their method works" (results). Those are very different epistemic statuses. key_sentence is a one-line LLM-extracted summary of the section — it lets you do fast section-level retrieval without pulling full chunks.
Chunk is the workhorse retrieval unit. It lives inside a Section. chunk_type classifies what the text does — is it defining a term, describing a procedure, reporting a number, stating a limitation? salience_score ranks how information-dense the chunk is — boilerplate ("in this paper we present") scores low, novel claims score high. The NEXT edge between consecutive chunks is subtle but powerful: when your RAG retrieves chunk 7, it can walk to chunks 6 and 8 to get context, without re-running a similarity search. This is graph traversal replacing vector lookup.

Layer 2: Content & knowledge entities — what chunks actually extract into. These are the nodes your RAG queries will hit most.The knowledge entities (Layer 2)
Concept represents a scientific idea — something that exists in the world of knowledge. Examples: "attention mechanism", "oxidative stress", "Bayesian inference", "tensile strength". Concepts don't belong to any one paper. They are shared across the literature. The aliases[] array is essential — "BERT" and "Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers" must resolve to the same Concept node, otherwise your graph has two islands that should be one. ontology links it to a controlled vocabulary like MeSH (medicine) or ChEBI (chemistry) so your graph speaks the same language as databases it might connect to.
Method is a procedure or technique that someone did. Examples: "fine-tuning", "X-ray crystallography", "Monte Carlo simulation", "gradient descent". The distinction from Concept matters: "attention" is a Concept; "multi-head self-attention with relative positional encoding" is a Method. Methods have a first_paper_id — the paper that introduced them — which is how you build lineage. type classifies the method category: algorithmic, experimental, statistical, computational.
Claim is the most important node in the entire graph and the one most systems skip. A Claim is the atomic unit of scientific knowledge — one falsifiable statement extracted from one chunk. Examples: "ResNet-50 achieves 76.1% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet", "the proposed method reduces inference time by 40% compared to the baseline", "the authors observe diminishing returns beyond 6 transformer layers". claim_type distinguishes finding (observed), hypothesis (proposed but untested), limitation (acknowledged weakness), and future_work (stated intention). confidence is the LLM's extraction confidence, not the scientific confidence — it tells you how sure the extractor was that it correctly identified this as a discrete claim. embedding lets you find semantically similar claims across papers, which is how you surface contradictions and replications.
Result is a quantitative Claim. It always has three anchors: the value itself, the Dataset it was measured on, and the Metric used. A Result without a Dataset is meaningless — "93% accuracy" tells you nothing without knowing it's on CIFAR-10 under a specific train/test split. condition captures experimental setup details like hardware, random seed, or preprocessing. This lets you query: "show me all F1 scores on SQuAD 2.0 from papers published after 2021" — a pure graph traversal that would be impossible without this structure.
Dataset is a shared measurement surface. Papers don't own datasets — multiple papers use the same one. This is why Dataset is its own node, not a field on Result. When you model it properly, you can ask "which method performs best on this dataset" by traversing all Results that have MEASURED_ON pointing to it. splits records whether the paper used the standard train/val/test split or a custom one — a detail that's often why two papers reporting on the same dataset can't be fairly compared.
Metric is how you measured. "Accuracy", "F1", "BLEU", "perplexity", "precision@k" are all Metrics. higher_is_better is a boolean that sounds trivial but matters when you're programmatically ranking results — you need to know whether to sort ascending or descending. category groups metrics: classification, generation, retrieval, efficiency.
Equation stores mathematical content. latex is the raw formula. plain_desc is a natural-language description like "cross-entropy loss between predicted and true distributions" — this is what makes equations searchable by non-experts. is_loss_fn is a targeted boolean because loss functions are a specific high-value query target in ML papers. domain classifies the equation's field: thermodynamics, probability theory, signal processing.
Figure / Table nodes store visual content. The key upgrades here are clip_embedding (a multimodal vector that lets you search figures by semantic similarity to text queries) and ocr (extracted text from axes, labels, and table cells). A results table in a paper is often the most information-dense thing in it — without OCR and structured extraction, it's invisible to your graph.



Layer 3: Semantic & provenance edges — the relationships that turn isolated nodes into a queryable knowledge graph. This is what makes GraphRAG actually work.

The semantic edges (Layer 3)
These are what separate a knowledge graph from a fancy document store. Edges carry meaning.
MENTIONS (Chunk → Concept/Method/Claim) is the extraction edge — it records that a chunk is the textual source of an entity. This is your audit trail. Every entity in your graph should have at least one MENTIONS edge pointing back to the chunk it came from. Without this you have floating facts with no provenance.
GROUNDED_IN (Claim → Chunk) is more specific than MENTIONS — it says "this Claim was extracted from this specific passage of text". This is what makes GraphRAG explainable. When your system answers a question, it can walk the graph backwards: Answer → Claim → Chunk → Section → Paper → display the source sentence. That chain is your citation.
IS_A (Method → Concept, or Method → Method) builds the ontology hierarchy. Transformer IS_A neural network. BERT IS_A transformer. This edge is what enables generalization queries — "find all papers using transformers" should return BERT, GPT, T5, and every other transformer variant even if the word "transformer" doesn't appear in the chunk.
IMPROVES (Method → Method) is the research lineage edge. It records that one method was explicitly designed to outperform another. This is how you build the evolutionary tree of a field — you can ask "trace the lineage of BERT" and walk the IMPROVES chain backwards through time.
SOLVES (Method → Problem) links a technique to the problem it addresses. This is the bridge that answers the most common research questions: "what methods exist for solving problem X?" or conversely "what problems does method Y address?". Without this edge those questions require full-text search. With it they're graph lookups.
USES (Method → Material, Method → Equation) records dependencies. A materials science method USES a specific reagent. An ML method USES a specific loss function. These edges let you find all methods that depend on a given resource — useful for reproducibility queries.
MEASURED_ON (Result → Dataset) and USING_METRIC (Result → Metric) are the two edges that make quantitative comparison possible. Together they say: "this number, measured this way, on this data". Without both edges, results from different papers can't be fairly compared because the measurement context is missing.
CITES (Paper → Paper) is the citation graph. On its own it's just bibliographic data. But combined with your knowledge entities it becomes powerful — you can traverse from a Claim, through its Paper, through CITES edges, to find the papers that built the foundation for that claim.
SUPPORTS / CONTRADICTS (Claim → Claim, cross-paper) are the highest-value edges in the entire schema. They record explicit epistemic relationships between claims from different papers. "Paper B's result contradicts Paper A's finding on the same dataset." This is what enables a GraphRAG system to answer synthesis questions like "what is the current scientific consensus on X?" or "which papers dispute finding Y?" — these are questions that are completely impossible with vector search alone, because they require understanding the relationship between statements, not just their similarity.
NEXT (Chunk → Chunk) is a sequential ordering edge within a section. It lets context reconstruction work as graph traversal rather than re-querying. Retrieve one chunk, walk forward and backward along NEXT edges to get its neighbours, and you have a coherent passage without any additional vector lookups.
Provenance on every edge — confidence, source_chunk_id, extractor_model — means every relationship in your graph is auditable. You always know how certain the extraction was, which passage it came from, and which model produced it. This is the difference between a research-grade system and a prototype: the ability to distrust and verify any individual fact in the graph.

Here's how to read the big picture:
Reading top to bottom maps directly to your RAG pipeline's data flow. A paper enters at the top, gets split into sections and chunks in Layer 1, those chunks get entity-extracted into Layer 2, and the semantic relationships and provenance get recorded in Layer 3.
Reading bottom to top maps to how a query traverses the graph. A user question hits a Claim or Result node, walks down to its Evidence link to find the source chunk, then up through Section → Paper to surface the citation. That full chain — answer to source sentence — lives entirely in the graph.
The three cross-layer edges are the ones worth paying attention to. First, MENTIONS / CONTAINS is the downward extraction edge from Chunk to every entity — it's how Layer 1 populates Layer 2. Second, GROUNDED_IN is the upward audit edge from Claim back to its source Chunk — it's how Layer 3 stays honest. Third, MAPS_TO links Concept and Method nodes to external ontology systems like MeSH or ChEBI — it's how your graph connects to the wider scientific knowledge ecosystem.
The CITES self-arc on Paper and the IMPROVES self-arc on Method are the lineage edges — they're what let you ask evolutionary questions like "trace the development of this technique over the last decade" by pure graph traversal rather than text search.
